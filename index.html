The Body as Dreamer

Mindful Movement & Depth Practice — Process, Not Perfection

Expressive Arts Therapy Group ◆ 75–90 minutes ◆ Adults

◆ Today's Intention

Today's group works with mindful movement as depth practice — the understanding that the body carries its own intelligence, its own memory, its own imaginal life. We are not using the body as an instrument of the mind. We are following the body as a guide in its own right. Movement researcher and somatic pioneer Emilie Conrad called the body "a verb, not a noun" — always in process, always becoming. That's our orientation today: process, not perfection. There is no wrong way to move. There is only the willingness to listen.

Principle I

Sensation before story — feel first, interpret later

Principle II

Follow the impulse — let movement arise rather than performing it

Principle III

Principle IV

◈ The Soma — Why the Body Matters in Depth Work

Western culture has long treated the body as a vehicle for the mind — something to manage, optimize, or transcend. Depth psychology challenges this split. The body is not separate from psyche; it is psyche in its most immediate, felt form. Emotions live in the gut, in the jaw, in the shoulders. Trauma is held in posture and breath pattern. And beneath all of it, the body is continuously generating its own imagery — gesture, rhythm, impulse, stillness — that carries meaning just as dreams do.

◆ Interoception — The Inner Sense

▸

Interoception is the sense that perceives the internal landscape of the body — heartbeat, breath, gut feeling, temperature, muscle tension, the felt sense of aliveness. It's the foundation of emotional awareness: we can't name what we feel until we sense what is happening inside us.

Research in affective neuroscience shows that interoception is the physiological ground of all emotion. When we tune into the body's inner signals, we're not doing something vague or mystical — we're accessing the primary data stream through which emotional life unfolds. Mindful movement is one of the most direct ways to cultivate this sense.

◈ The Body Holds the Story

▸

Somatic psychology and trauma research both point to the same insight: experience is stored in the body. Not as a metaphor, but as pattern — in breath holding, muscular bracing, postural collapse, chronic tension, frozen gestures. The body is a living archive of everything we've been through.

Mindful movement offers a way to access this material not through analysis but through felt engagement. When we slow down and follow the body's impulse rather than directing it, what surfaces often surprises us. A shoulder drops and grief arrives. A fist unclenches and something loosens in the chest. The body doesn't need to be told what it's carrying — it needs to be given space to speak.

◇ Movement as Imaginal Practice

▸

The depth tradition recognizes that the imaginal doesn't only arrive in mental images — it arrives in the body's own gestures, rhythms, and postures. When we move without a plan and stay present to what unfolds, we are practicing a form of active imagination through the soma. The movement is the image.

Dance therapist Mary Whitehouse, who bridged depth psychology with movement, described this as "moving from the inside" — not imposing choreography on the body but letting the body reveal what psyche is doing beneath the surface. The goal is not beautiful movement. The goal is authentic movement, whatever that looks like.

◇ Polyvagal Awareness

▸

Polyvagal theory gives us a map of the nervous system's three primary states: ventral vagal (social engagement, safety, connection), sympathetic (mobilization, fight-or-flight, activation), and dorsal vagal (immobilization, shutdown, conservation). These aren't just abstract categories — they are felt states that shape our posture, movement quality, and capacity for engagement.

Mindful movement can help us notice which state we're in without judgment, and gently expand our capacity to move between them. Today you may notice your body wanting to be still (dorsal), wanting to move quickly (sympathetic), or wanting to connect and reach outward (ventral). All of these are valid starting points. The practice is simply to notice, not to fix.

◇ Ground Rules for Today

◆ No performing. This is not choreography, fitness, or presentation. You are the only audience.

◆ Process over product. The quality of attention matters more than the quality of movement.

◆ Honor your body. Pain is a boundary. Modify, pause, or sit any station out. Stillness is always a full participation option.

◆ Eyes soft or closed. Closing your eyes can deepen inward attention. If that doesn't feel safe, a soft downward gaze works just as well.

◆ Confidentiality. What's shared in group stays in group.

◆ Grounding is always available. If intensity arises, return to your feet on the floor, your breath, the sensation of your hands touching each other.

◆ Movement Stations

Choose one or two stations that call to you. Each station can be done standing, seated, or lying down — adapt to what your body needs. Spend 15–25 minutes in your chosen station. Click a station to expand its full instructions.

Remember: stillness is its own depth practice. You can always sit quietly with eyes closed and attend inward.

Station I

Following the Impulse

Authentic Movement — Moving from the Inside

Close your eyes. Wait for the body to initiate. Follow whatever movement wants to come — no choreography, no plan.

Entry: Stand or sit with eyes closed. Let your weight settle. Rather than deciding to move, wait for movement to find you. It might begin as a shift of weight, a tilt of the head, a turning of a wrist. It may take a minute. That waiting is part of the practice.

Engagement: Follow the impulse wherever it leads. If your hand wants to rise, let it. If your torso wants to curl, follow it. If nothing moves, stay with the stillness — attend to the micro-movements inside: your heartbeat, your breath, the subtle sway of standing. You are not performing movement. You are being moved. If the inner critic arrives ("this looks weird," "I'm doing it wrong"), notice the voice, let it pass, and return to sensation.

Landing: When the movement feels complete — or when you're called to stop — return to stillness gradually. Take three breaths. Notice what changed. Then, on paper, draw the shape of the movement you just experienced — not a figure drawing, but an abstract trace: a line, a spiral, a series of marks. Or write one sentence: "My body said…"

This station draws directly from the Authentic Movement tradition (Mary Whitehouse, Janet Adler). If possible, offer a "witness" option — one person moves while another sits with eyes open, holding compassionate attention. The mover moves; the witness receives. Debrief with "I saw…" (witness) and "I felt…" (mover), keeping interpretation to a minimum.

Station II

Breath as Choreographer

Breath-Led Movement

Let your breath set the pace, the size, and the quality of every movement. The body follows the breath, not the other way around.

Entry: Begin standing or seated. Close your eyes. Take several natural breaths without changing anything. Simply observe: where does the breath move in your body? Does it fill the chest? The belly? Does it reach the back of the ribs? The pelvis? Notice the shape breath makes in you.

Engagement: Now let the breath begin to grow slightly. On each inhale, let the body expand — in whatever direction it wants. On each exhale, let it soften, release, fold. Don't plan the shape. Let the breath decide what the body does. A deep inhale may lift your arms. A long exhale may bow your spine. Short, quick breaths may produce shaking or fluttering. Trust it. Spend 10–15 minutes here, letting the breath choreograph your body through its own intelligence.

Landing: Return to natural, quiet breath. Let your body come to stillness on its own timeline. When you arrive, place both hands on your abdomen. Notice the quality of your breath now versus when you started. Write or sketch: "The breath took me to…"

Ambient, non-rhythmic soundscapes work well here — ocean, wind, resonant bowls. Avoid music with a beat, which imposes external rhythm. Remind participants that hyperventilation is a sign to slow down and return to natural pace. This station is particularly accessible for those new to movement work.

Station III

Weight & Yield

Gravity, Support, Surrender

Explore the relationship between your body and the ground — the primal conversation between weight, support, and letting go.

Entry: Begin standing. Feel your full weight pressing into the floor through the soles of your feet. Slowly begin to give more weight to one side, then the other. Let yourself sway. Notice where you brace, where you resist falling, where you trust the floor to hold you.

Engagement: Gradually lower yourself toward the ground — at whatever pace feels right. You might fold, kneel, sit, or lie down fully. Explore what it's like to yield weight rather than collapse. There's a difference between falling and surrendering. As you arrive on the floor, feel the surface supporting you. What happens when you stop holding yourself up? Where does the body release? Where does it still brace? Roll, curl, spread, press into the floor. Let it be a conversation between you and gravity.

Rising: When you're ready, begin finding your way back to standing — but slowly, like growing. Notice the effort of rising. Notice what it takes to reclaim vertical. Pause at any level that interests you: floor, kneeling, half-standing, standing. When you arrive upright, feel the difference. Write or draw: one word for what you released; one word for what you reclaimed.

This station draws from developmental movement patterns (Bonnie Bainbridge Cohen, Body-Mind Centering). Provide yoga mats or blankets for comfort on the floor. Seated participants can explore yielding weight into a chair, a wall, or their own hands. The metaphor of support and surrender often resonates deeply for participants carrying burdens or struggling with control.

Station IV

The Gesture That Speaks

Symbolic Movement & Repetition

Find a single gesture — a reaching, a turning away, a closing, an opening — and repeat it until it reveals its deeper meaning.

Entry: Stand or sit. Bring to mind a feeling, a situation, or a question that is alive for you right now. Don't overthink — take the first thing that arrives. Now let your body find one gesture that expresses something about that feeling. It might be a hand pressing outward (boundary). A reaching forward (longing). Arms wrapping around yourself (protection). A turning of the head (refusal or searching). Let it be instinctive.

Engagement: Repeat the gesture. Slowly. Again and again. Each repetition, notice what shifts — does the gesture grow? Shrink? Change quality? Does it want to transform into something else? Stay with the repetition long enough for it to stop being about what you decided and start becoming about what it wants to say. This is the body's version of active imagination — a gesture repeated until it deepens into revelation.

Landing: Let the gesture arrive at a final form — its most essential version. Hold it for three breaths like a living sculpture. Then release. Write: "This gesture is about…" and "What it needed me to know is…"

This station has strong emotional potential. The repetition of a gesture can surface grief, anger, tenderness, or longing unexpectedly. Normalize whatever arises. Pairs work well here: one person repeats their gesture while the other witnesses, then switch. The witness doesn't interpret — they simply share what they noticed ("I saw your hand slow down near the end").

Station V

Rhythm, Shake, Release

Activation, Discharge, Reset

Use shaking, rhythmic bouncing, and percussive movement to discharge held tension and let the nervous system reset.

Entry: Stand with feet hip-width apart, knees slightly bent. Begin bouncing gently — not jumping, just letting your knees soften and spring. Feel the vibration travel through your whole body. Let your arms hang loose, your jaw soften, your shoulders drop. Increase the bounce slowly.

Engagement: Now begin to shake. Shake your hands. Your arms. Your legs. Your whole body. This isn't exercise — it's neurological discharge. Animals in the wild shake after a threat to release survival energy. Humans store it. Let your body do what it already knows how to do. Shake for 5–7 minutes. It may feel silly at first, then intense, then strangely calming. If sound wants to come — a sigh, a groan, a laugh — let it.

Landing: Slow the shaking gradually. Come to complete stillness. Stand or lie down. Feel the aliveness in your body — the tingling, the warmth, the subtle vibration that continues even after you stop. This is your nervous system reorganizing. Stay here for 2–3 minutes in silence. Then write: what did your body release? What arrived in the space that was cleared?

This station is informed by Trauma Release Exercises (TRE) and somatic experiencing principles. Drumming or rhythmic music supports this station well. Watch for participants who dissociate during shaking (glazed eyes, checking out) — gently invite them to feel their feet, slow down, or come to standing stillness. The discharge process can be activating; ensure grounding time after.

Station VI

The Space Between

Relational Movement — Mirroring & Distance

Explore movement in relationship to another person — the dance of approach, distance, attunement, and autonomy.

Entry: Find a partner. Stand facing each other at a comfortable distance. Take a moment to simply be in each other's presence without performing or filling the silence. Notice what your body does when it's being seen — does it tighten? Soften? Look away?

Engagement — Part 1, Mirroring: One person begins moving slowly. The other follows, mirroring as closely as possible — not mimicking, but attuning. The leader moves from inner impulse; the follower practices empathic sensing. After 3–4 minutes, switch roles. Then move into a phase where neither leads — both respond to each other simultaneously.

Engagement — Part 2, Distance: Still facing each other, begin to explore proximity. Slowly move closer. Notice where your body says "enough." Move apart. Notice where longing or relief arises. Find the distance that feels right — then notice it may keep changing. This is the somatic experience of attachment: the ongoing negotiation between closeness and space.

Landing: Return to your original distance. Offer a small gesture of acknowledgment — a nod, a hand on heart, a bow. Write privately: "Near you, my body felt… Far from you, my body felt…"

This station touches attachment patterns directly. Some participants may find closeness activating; others may struggle with distance. Normalize all responses. If the group has odd numbers, offer a solo version: move in relationship to an empty chair or a point in the room. The solo exploration of approach/withdrawal is equally rich.

Station VII

The Open Gate

Intuitive — Your Own Practice or Deep Stillness

Trust whatever your body is asking for. Move, be still, combine stations, or rest in deep listening.

Entry: The depth tradition recognizes that each body has its own way of processing. Your body may be asking for something none of the other stations cover. Or it may be asking for nothing — for rest, for stillness, for the simple experience of being held by gravity in a room where you don't have to produce anything.

Engagement: Follow what arises. You might combine elements — shake and then lie down, mirror yourself in the wall's shadow, repeat a gesture while breathing deeply. Or you might lie on the floor with your eyes closed and simply practice interoception: sensing the internal weather of your body with no agenda to change it. Receptive awareness is one of the deepest movement practices there is.

Capture: However you spent this time, leave a trace. A word, a drawing, a posture you want to remember. Something small to carry the body's message back into the verbal world.

This station functions as the "pass with full presence" option. Lying in constructive rest (on back, knees bent, feet flat) is encouraged as an alternative. Covering with a blanket can help participants who need extra containment. Emphasize that rest is not opting out — it is a somatic practice in its own right.

◇ Integration & Reflection

Take a few minutes to transition from body time back to verbal time. This transition is important — the body speaks one language, and words speak another. Integration is the bridge between them. Don't rush it.

Landing in the Body

Before reflecting in words, take 30 seconds to simply stand or sit and scan. How does your body feel now compared to when you arrived? Don't look for the right answer. Just notice temperature, weight, tension, openness, aliveness, fatigue — whatever is present. This noticing is the integration beginning.

Reflection Prompts

Choose one or two that resonate. You're welcome to write, share aloud, or hold your reflection in the body where it lives.

Invite voluntary sharing — pairs first (2–3 minutes each), then the wider group. Participants can share in words, or they can share a gesture. Offering "you can show us instead of tell us" opens a powerful alternative channel. Normalize all responses, including "I felt nothing" or "I didn't move much." Both carry information. Resistance and emptiness are not failures — they are somatic data.

◈ Discussion Seeds

For group processing after individual reflection:

◆ What was it like to move without a plan — to follow the body's impulse rather than direct it? Where was that easy, and where did you resist?

◆ The depth tradition says the body "dreams" through its gestures. Did your movement feel like it was telling you something? What?

◆ Where do you notice the process vs. product tension in your relationship with your own body — in exercise, appearance, performance, health? What would it mean to listen to the body's intelligence rather than manage it?

◆ What did you notice about your nervous system today — activation, stillness, shutdown, aliveness? No state is wrong. They're all chapters of the body's story.
